smaller teams are better. if you've ever built something really cool you probably did it by yourself or with a group of <5 people. beyond that, coordination and communication costs get so high that you need to constantly be optimizing against them to make meaningful progress.

for a long time the way i've wanted to work is as a pseudo-consultancy: a team of ~4 people with a range of skills (software, design, ops) who can move incredibly quickly and move from problem to problem (or company to company). 

when i've left jobs, there's always a handful of people who i _truly_ regret not being able to work with before. i want to grab those people, stick them to the katamari-damacy-blob, and roll them with me to the next job, where we'll have even _more_ success building together. 

i think AI/LLMs are going to bring make this working model obvious. if you've spent time building software with LLMs you'll realize that coordinating between agents/sessions over time is still one of the most challenging parts. you're constantly optimizing - orchestrator agents to manage others, new types of context docs, limiting what tools need to be available - just to keep allow the actual building work to proceed.
